# The Scribe's First Day

They warn you, when you join the Amaranthine Watch, that the first day is the
hardest. Ferren had assumed they meant the paperwork.

The hospice outside Haven's Rest is small and whitewashed and smells of lemon
balm, and the Backward Saint was in the garden when the senior scribe brought him
through, and she looked up from her beans, and her whole face broke open.

"*Ferren*," she said, and she was across the garden before he could bow, and she
took his hands in both of hers like something recovered from a flood. She was
crying. She was laughing. She kept touching his face the way grandmothers do.
"Look at you. Look at you, so *young* again. Oh — oh, I forgot how young you
start."

"Sister," he managed. "We haven't — I'm new. We haven't met."

"I know," she said, weeping, beaming. "I know exactly. You haven't met me. And I
will never see you again. Both of those, at once — you'll get used to it,
love, you'll be so patient with me later. *Earlier.*" She pressed his knuckles to
her forehead, which is what pilgrims do at shrines, and it is a strange thing to
have done to you by a saint in a bean garden.

The senior scribe, Amma, explained it that evening, the way it was once explained
to her. Sister Amaranth lives the other way. His first day is her last day of
him. Every day he serves, she will know him a little less. In his final week —
years from now, whenever his rotation ends — she will pass him in the corridor
with the mild, courteous look you give a stranger, and that is how he will know
it's time to go.

"So today," Amma said, "you got all of it. The whole friendship, ending. She's
been losing you as long as she's known you. Tomorrow will be easier. For her,
tomorrow you're nearly gone; for you, you'll barely have begun. You meet in the
middle, roughly. It's the middle years that are almost normal."

"That's monstrous," said Ferren.

"It's Tuesday," said Amma. "Log your entries in ink."

He couldn't sleep, so he was in the scriptorium after midnight when the Saint
shuffled in, small in her nightgown, and looked at him a long moment with the
particular tenderness of the very end of things.

"You'll fix the gate hinge," she said. "Not soon. The winter after the wet one.
Everyone will have stopped noticing the sound it makes except you." She patted
his arm. "Thank you for that. I wanted to say it while I still knew to."

He wrote it down, because that is the whole of the job: *write it down*. Filed,
witnessed, dated. Entry one of, the seniors assured him, several thousand.

At the door she paused, not quite turning.

"When it all begins," she said, in the pleasant tone of a woman remarking on
weather, "stand behind me. I checked. It helps."

Then she went to bed, and left him alone with the ink drying on the strangest
sentence he had ever written, which the manuals required him to classify, and
which he filed — after a long time, hand hovering — under *kindness, probable*.
