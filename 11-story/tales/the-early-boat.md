# The Early Boat

The *Held Promise* came home on Tuesday. She wasn't due to leave until Thursday.

Isla was on the dock when it happened, because on the reef coast someone is
always on the dock; that is what the dock is for. The boat came in through the
morning haze with the tide behind it, riding low the way a boat rides when the
hold is full and the season has been kind. Her husband's boat. Her husband was at
that moment asleep in their bed, two streets up the hill, with Thursday's nets
already stacked by the door.

You do not hail an early boat. Every child on the coast learns it with their
letters: *do not call to it, do not wave, do not say any name aloud.* The sea has
already decided what the early boat carries; all you may do is look, and count,
and hold your face still.

Isla looked. She counted.

The catch was good — low in the water, gulls interested. The port rail was
broken, a raw pale scar of snapped wood near the stern. And there were four
figures working the deck.

Five had signed for Thursday's sailing.

She did not cry out. She held her face still, the way her mother had taught her,
the way her mother's mother had taught *her*, and she counted again, and it was
four again, and then the *Held Promise* passed behind the breakwater light and
was not there anymore, and the water where it had been was just water.

The law of the early boat is that you may prepare, but you may not prevent. Boats
that stayed home have come back early anyway, and worse. So on Thursday she stood
on the dock with the other families and watched five of them board — her husband;
his brother; the two Ostrey boys; and cousin Marek, laughing, who had rolled his
shoulder at her and said the rail on the port side wanted replacing after this
run anyway, and had she heard, the reef-lights were mild this season.

She told her husband about the rail. Custom permits the rail. She did not tell
him the count. Custom is silent on the count, and she has wondered every day
since what silence is *for*.

The *Held Promise* came home on the following Tuesday, riding low, kind season,
hold full. The port rail was broken. Marek had gone over in the night swell off
the reefs with a line fouled round the winch, quick and quiet, the way the sea
prefers to do its bookkeeping.

At the burial-at-sea they gave him to Naveris, and Isla stood at the stern with
the widow Ostrey, who is her friend, and who held her hand and said the thing
the reef coast always says, the thing that is a comfort and a verdict and a
question that has never once been answered:

"It was already Tuesday for him, love. It had been Tuesday all week."

Marek's Life-Debt passed to his sister's boy, as debts do. The rail was replaced
with good red teak. And Isla still goes down to the dock each morning, because
someone is always on the dock, and she counts every boat that comes in, early or
on time or late, and she holds her face still, and she has never yet worked out
whether the counting is how you honor them or how you kill them.
