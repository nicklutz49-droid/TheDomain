# Deposition D-41

*Union Bureau of Anomalous Events, deposition of walker 17 of 41, taken nine days
after the Night of Doors. Deponent: Limara Vey, tram-mechanic, Verantum Third
District. Examiner's annotations preserved.*

---

**Q. State what happened in your own words.**

A. I was up late because the kettle was on. My pantry door was open and it
shouldn't have been, and the light coming out of it was wrong — pantries are
dark. It was a corridor. White, but not cold white. Like paper in lamplight.

**Q. Did you feel compelled to enter?**

A. No. That's what nobody wants to hear, but no. It was like a door held open by
someone polite. You know how a held door makes you hurry a little? Not magic.
Manners. I went in because it would have been strange not to.

**Q. Describe the corridor.**

A. Long. Warm underfoot, like a floor over a kitchen. A hum, one note, low —
my tram hums like that when the circuit's happy, so I wasn't scared. Doors down
both sides. I counted forty before I stopped counting.

*[Examiner's note: walker 9 counted twelve doors. Walker 31 counted "hundreds."
Walker 24 reports windows. No two counts agree.]*

**Q. Did you open any doors?**

A. Touched one. It felt like weather. Like the pressure change before rain, but a
door. I didn't open it. It wasn't for me. I don't know how I knew that, but you
know whose coat is whose on a rack, don't you.

**Q. Did you encounter other individuals?**

A. A man. Older, tidy, ink on his fingers. We nodded like you do on a night tram.
He was walking slow, reading the doors — not reading, but you know. *Listening
with his eyes.* When I turned back he was still going. He stopped at one and put
his hand flat on it and smiled, and he said — not to me, to himself — he said,
"this one's mine."

**Q. For the record, you have identified this man as Danel Ost.**

A. From the picture, yes. That's him that's missing? He didn't look lost, sir. I
want that written down. Whatever door that was, it was his. He looked like a man
coming home from a very long shift.

**Q. Why did you leave the corridor?**

A. My kettle. You'll laugh. But the corridor didn't mind. That's the other thing
nobody wants written down. When I stepped back through, the hum went down a
little tone, like — like it was sorry to see me go, but glad I'd come. The
corridor was *glad*, sir. I've kept the pantry door oiled ever since. It's never
been anything but a pantry again. But I keep it oiled.
