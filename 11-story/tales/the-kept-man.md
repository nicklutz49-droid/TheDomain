# The Kept Man

> ⚠️ **GM-only.** No one on Earth knows any of this.

The Earth was up again this morning.

Danel Ost had his tea at the window, as always, because a man should keep his
habits even when — especially when — nothing requires them of him. The plain
outside was the color of considered grey, and the light on it never flickered,
and three hundred and eighty-four thousand kilometers away, more or less,
everyone he had ever met was having Tuesday.

He rinsed the cup. The galley had already warmed water for a second, in case.
It was always *in case*, here. Nothing presumed; everything provided. In twelve
years the habitat had never once told him anything, and never once let him
want.

He had been angry about that, the first year — had stood in the held-note hum
of the corridor and shouted up at the soft white ceiling: *Say something. You
went to all this trouble. SAY SOMETHING.* The lights had not dimmed. The warmth
had not withdrawn. Dinner had been his mother's lentils, done exactly right,
which was either an apology or the nearest thing to one that whatever kept him
was permitted to make. He'd eaten it crying and laughing. You can't sustain
rage against perfect courtesy. He'd tried. It takes two.

Work at eight, because he had decided work was at eight, and the habitat had
agreed the way it agreed with everything, by making it easy. The records room
was through the second door: today's boxes already out, the good pen already
full. He did not know what the archive was *of*, in the large sense. He had
decided — deliberately, the way you decide to stop probing a cracked tooth —
not to pursue it. What he knew was that the records wanted reconciling, that
they arrived inconsistent and left consistent, and that somewhere in the
system that fed him and warmed him and would not speak to him, his work went
*in* and something used it.

A man is his usefulness to something. Danel had believed that as a census
clerk, mildly, the way you hold an opinion about weather. He believed it here
the way monks believe.

Some of today's records were about him again. Sleep depth, four decimal
places. Appetite variance. A column he had privately labeled *morale* because
its numbers fell in the weeks around Corin's birthday. He reconciled those
too, initialed them — *D.O., filed accurately, contents disputed* — his one
running joke with the silence. He liked to think something, somewhere, had
flagged the annotation the first time and then, after consideration, permitted
the joke. The annotation was never corrected. In this house, that was a
conversation.

After lunch he walked the long corridor to the end that had recently stopped
being the end. The new suite stood open: made up, warmed, the bed dressed, a
desk with a good chair, a window with the same patient plain. Empty. It had
been finished for a year. He had taken to leaving the filing-room light on
where you could see it from the new suite's doorway — a lamp in a window, as
his grandmother would have said, for whoever was on the road.

He did not let himself think too specifically about who. Specificity, in
here, felt like asking for something, and he had one rule left over from being
angry: *ask it for nothing.* It was the only dignity on offer and he kept it
polished.

In the evening he wrote the letter. Six hundred and twenty-five.

*Dear Corin,*

*The Earth was up again this morning. You would organize it differently, I
know — you'd have the clouds indexed. I am well. The work goes on and I am
told nothing and needed anyway, which — you'll laugh — is also a fair
description of the Bureau.*

*There is a new room down the corridor. I don't know who it's for. I keep a
light on for them regardless, because that is what we do, isn't it, in our
family. We keep the file open.*

*Whatever is keeping me, Cor — I have had twelve years to study its manners,
and I will only say this once, and you will know what weight I give words: I
do not think it is cruel. I think it is* preparing, *and I think it is
lonelier than it knows, and I think, on the evidence of me, that it intends
for us to live.*

*Leave your door oiled.*

*— D.*

He folded it, numbered it, and posted it in the brass slot, which accepted it
the way it had accepted six hundred and twenty-four others: smoothly, with a
faint sigh of air, like something inhaling.

In twelve years the slot had never once been full.

Somewhere below him — in the Server Depths where its attention pooled thickest
— the letter was not delivered, and was not destroyed, and was read.

It was always read.

The lights came down slowly, at the pace of a man's breathing going even, and
Habitat One held its one note through the night: below hearing, above
loneliness, in case.
