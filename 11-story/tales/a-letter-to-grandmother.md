# A Letter to Grandmother

*Volume II of the Threefold Testimony. The author, Suli Maren-daughter, was eight
years old. Provenance sealed; spelling preserved.*

---

Dear Grandmother,

You said write you everything about festival day so I am writing EVERYTHING even
the parts Mama says I remember wrong.

We went to Audit Square very early to get a good spot. Papa put me on his
shoulders. The scales-god was there, the one from the market, Quorion, he blesses
the weights so nobody cheats. You met him once you said. You said he was boring.
Grandmother he was NOT boring.

He had a machine. It was like Auntie's loom but made of the shiny circuit metal,
and he stood next to it and he said, watch, I will do the blessing WITHOUT my
hands, and the machine did the blessing. The bread rose up golden just the same
as when he does it. And he said, do you see, it is not a miracle, it is a
MECHANISM, and he sounded so happy, like when I showed you I could do my letters.
He said, you can all inspect it. Some people went up and looked inside the
machine. A man started crying. I don't know why, the bread was fine.

Then everyone was shouting and Papa's shoulders went stiff and then the beautiful
ones came. Mama says I have to call them the Twelve but there were only three of
them, so.

Grandmother they are so tall. Not tall like Papa. Tall like when you look down a
well but upside down.

The gray one with the white eyes talked and his voice came from everywhere like
the square itself was talking. He said Quorion had reached PERFECT UNDERSTANDING
and now he would ascend and be with the divine forever. And everyone cheered and
Mama cried and there was so much light.

But Grandmother here is the part Mama says I remember wrong. I was up high on
Papa's shoulders so I could see over everyone. Quorion did not look happy like
when he showed the machine. He looked like Tam looks when the big boys come and
Tam pretends he was leaving anyway. And when the light came, it did not go UP.
Everyone says he rose up. Grandmother he did not rise up. His light went IN, like
water down a drain, like when the tub empties, and the machine light went in
too, and then the square was just a square and everyone was still cheering.

They gave us honey bread after. I kept my ribbon. Mama says it was the most
beautiful thing she ever saw and I asked her, then why did the god look scared,
and she got angry, so now I only say it to you.

Papa says next year I am big enough to hold the lantern at the Audit.

Please come for Still Days. I miss you. Tell me if the bread rises different in
the country.

Your Suli
