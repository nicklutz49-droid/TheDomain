# What the Widow Kept

The report said: *possible manifestation, Class I, Hollen Cross, cellar of the
Marn house. Neighbors report light.* Class I means one specialist, no escort, and
so Aldous went alone, which was how he preferred it, because the escorts always
wanted to *do* something, and Class I work is mostly about not doing things.

The widow Marn gave him tea first. That told him most of it. Nobody with a
Gnawer in the cellar makes tea.

"He comes at lamplighting," she said, hands folded. "You'll want to wait."

Her husband Tobin had been dead a year and a season — the Vigil kept, the
Speaking spoken, the ashes vaulted properly in town, everything done right, she
was very clear on that, everything done *right*. And at lamplighting the cellar
air went silver, and there he was on the stair: translucent, faintly luminous, a
big man in a mended coat, forever coming up from checking the apples.

Echoes are the gentlest thing the Domain does wrong. They don't see you. They
replay. Tobin's Echo said three things, in order, always: *"Frost tonight, I
shouldn't wonder."* Then, at the top of the stair, wiping his hands: *"The brown
hen's off her feed again."* Then, turning, to no one, with a smile the specialist
recognized because his own father had owned one like it: *"Don't wait up, love.
I'll bank the fire."*

Then silver, then nothing, until tomorrow's lamplighting.

"The manual language," Aldous said carefully, when they were upstairs again, "is
that Echoes present a psychological danger."

"I know what the temple says." She refilled his cup, steady as a magistrate.
"That he's an error. That the soul disperses, and this is just — the Domain
stuttering. A picture that fell out of the world's pocket." She looked at him
then, and her eyes were dry and formidable. "Young man, I sat the Vigil. I felt
him go. I know that's not my Tobin. You'll not find me down there talking to it,
setting a place for it. I'm not the sort that goes strange."

"Then—"

"But it has his *hands*," she said, and for one second the magistrate cracked
clean across, "and it banks a fire that isn't there so I won't be cold, and I am
asking you, before all your forms and your circles: whom does it harm?"

The correct answer is in the manual. Prolonged exposure. Arrested grieving. The
documented cases: the ones who stop leaving the house, who wave off the living to
keep appointments with the silver. Psychological danger only — *only*, the manual
says, as if the mind were a shed and not the whole of the estate.

Aldous went down at lamplighting and watched the whole replay with his
instruments dark. Frost tonight. The brown hen. Don't wait up, love.

In his ledger, in the space for findings, he wrote: *No manifestation warranting
dissolution at this time. Recommend periodic observation.* Which was not quite a
lie and not quite the truth, and he has driven out the third Sunday of every
month since — to observe, the ledger says. The widow makes tea. At lamplighting
they stand together at the top of the cellar stair, not talking to it, not
setting a place for it, neither of them strange, and the silver man wipes his
hands and tells them both not to wait up.

The manual is right, is the thing. Aldous knows exactly what the danger is now.
It isn't that the Echo wants anything. It's that it doesn't — and the living do,
and a fire that was banked for you every night for forty years is a very hard
thing to agree has stopped existing.

He'll dissolve it when she's gone. He's promised himself. It's in the ledger,
undated.
