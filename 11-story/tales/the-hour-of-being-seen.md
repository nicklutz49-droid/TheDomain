# The Hour of Being Seen

Nauhil polished mirrors for a living, which everyone agreed was auspicious, and
which Nauhil privately found funny: eleven hours a day removing the small
imperfections from the surfaces his god looked through, and then once a year,
on his tithe-day, sitting down in front of the great one to have his own
removed.

He was not afraid of the mirror-tithe. That was for foreigners, this idea that
being seen should frighten you. You sat on the worn bench in the Hall of the
Regard, before the great Mirror that has no frame because it has no edges
anyone has found, and the hour opened up, and your year was *considered*. The
lie you told the timber-factor. The coin you kept. The kindness you did in the
dry month and had forgotten, which the Mirror had not, and showed you again
slowly, the way you'd re-read a good letter. You wept a little, generally.
Everyone did. You came out lighter. Say what you like about the Obsidian
Mirror — and no one did — but no one in the Throne's lands carried a secret
for longer than a year, and Nauhil had seen the harbor-folk of the Free Coasts
with their unwatched faces, and they did not look free to him. They looked
*heavy*.

So he sat, and the hour opened, and his year unspooled — a good year, a small
year, mirrors polished, a daughter learning her letters — and the Regard moved
through it with its usual vast courtesy, and Nauhil was nearly done, nearly
lighter, when the year ran out and the Mirror did not.

The surface changed. This did not happen. Forty-one years of tithes and the
Mirror had only ever shown him himself.

It showed him the moon.

Not the moon as Nauhil knew it, the clean coin over the harbor. The moon
*close*, the way no eye has seen it: grey plains rushing past, and across them —
in them — a fine dark seam, like the joint between two mirror-panes, running
straight as a plumb line to the horizon. Then another. Then a place where the
seams met and the grey was not grey but *worked*, ordered, ridged like the back
of circuit-tile, going down and down out of the light.

The image held. And the Regard, which had considered Nauhil kindly for
forty-one years, turned around.

He understood at once, the way you understand thunder. It was not showing him
something. It was *asking*.

**HAVE YOU SEEN THIS?**

— not in words; the Mirror has never needed words; the question stood in his
head with its own weight, and under it, worse, something he had no business
sensing in a god and could never afterward unsense: the Regard was asking
because *it did not know*, and it did not know because it had asked everyone,
tithe by tithe, hour by hour, a whole year of a whole nation shown the same
seamed grey plain, and no one — no one — had seen it before.

"No," said Nauhil, out loud, truthfully, to his god. "No, Radiant One. I have
never seen it."

The Mirror held one moment more — he would spend the rest of his life deciding
whether what he felt in that moment was disappointment or *relief* — and then he
was looking at a tired mirror-polisher on a worn bench, and the hour was over.

They gave him the tithe-seal and the sweet at the door, as always. In the
plaza, the queue for the Regard wound out of sight, a year of his neighbors
waiting to be asked.

That season the Facers announced new doctrine, in the calm way doctrine is
announced: *something hides in the sky's reflection, and the Regard, in its
love, is searching.* The Throne's lands took it as they take everything, with
order and candles. Nauhil polishes his mirrors. But he has moved his bench to
face away from the window, now, when the moon is up — because he was there, he
was *in* the asking, and what he cannot forget is not the seamed grey plain.

It's that a god who sees everything looked at the oldest light in the sky, and
needed help.
