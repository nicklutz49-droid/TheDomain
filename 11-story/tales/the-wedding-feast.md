# The Wedding Feast

Merta took the early train up from Weissbrunn with a speech prepared. Someone in
the family had to give it, and the aunts had all discovered pressing obligations
elsewhere. *Grandfather, it has been fifty years. It is a cupboard, not a chapel.
Let the priest bless the thing and carry it away, and be done.*

The letter that summoned them had gone out to every branch of the Brandts in
Josef's upright ledger hand: *On Templeday it will be fifty years to the day.
The last course of my wedding will now be served. Come hungry.*

Everyone in the district knew the story; it was the polite scandal of three
villages. Fifty years ago a storm came down off the hills in the middle of Josef
and Liesel's wedding feast — the marquee lifting like a live thing, the lamps
out, half the guests soaked and the other half chasing tablecloths across a
field. The last course never left the kitchen. The cooks, being sensible women,
sealed it in the household [stasis unit](../../08-civilization/daily-life.md)
"for later."

Later had now lasted half a century.

In the Empire you do not keep food from mouths. [Leaving food insults host,
house, and god](../../08-civilization/cuisine.md); a whole course held uneaten
for fifty years sat on the exact edge of sin, and the neighbors had muttered
about it for decades. A priest came out twice that Merta knew of, and twice went
home unsure what, precisely, to cite — the course was not spoiling, and it had
not been refused. Josef's answer never varied, to priests, to neighbors, to his
own daughters: "It is not waste. It is kept."

He was not, she had learned since, the only one. Every district has a household
or two like it — a **kept feast**, the thing is called where anyone admits it is
a custom at all: the last cooking of the dead, or of the day everything changed,
sealed behind a stasis field and held. The temple has never ruled on whether it
is waste. Nobody wants to be the priest who rules on it.

Liesel died eight years ago, and it was all done right — the Vigil sat, [the
Speaking](../../08-civilization/death-and-afterlife.md) spoken, her ashes vaulted
with the Brandt dead. Josef went on. He kept the garden, wound the clocks, and
did not speak of the pantry, and nobody made him.

Now the house was full of Brandts — four generations, the youngest of whom had
never met her and the rest of whom had mostly been children when she went. Her
chair stood at the table's left hand, set with the others. Josef, ninety-one and
straight as a fence post, opened the pantry door and laid his hand on the panel,
and the field let go with a sound like a held breath released.

Steam rose. That was the thing nobody was ready for, though every one of them
had grown up with stasis crocks and knew the physics as well as anyone knows
them. The bride-cake came out warm from an oven fifty years cold. The candied
plums shone. The sweet dark wine stood already poured in its glasses, trayed and
ready to carry, the way the cooks had left it when the lamps went out — fifty
years old and eight minutes old at once, and the room went very quiet.

Josef cut Liesel's portion first and set it at her place. Then, as at a
Speaking, it was divided out — a spoonful to every plate down the table, and
with each spoonful a memory, said aloud. The old ones had their own. The young
ones gave borrowed ones, solemn as coins: "She could whistle two notes at once,"
said a boy who had it from his mother, who had it from hers. Merta, when the
spoon reached her, said that her grandmother ate standing up at her own stove
her whole life, like a woman with somewhere to be, and was surprised to find
she could not quite say it levelly.

The toast had been on the tray all along — the folded paper Josef had carried in
his pocket that night, tucked under his own glass when the storm broke, sealed
in with everything else. He opened it, and the creases were still new. He read
the first line aloud — *"To the long life we are going to have"* — and stopped,
and folded it away.

"I was twenty-three," he said. "I can improve on it now. To the long life we
had."

They drank the wedding wine to a woman most of the room had never met, and then
they ate all of it, down to the sugar-work, the smallest children coached
through the sacrament of the clean plate. Nothing kept. Nothing wasted. When it
was done the pantry stood open and dark, the unit's hum stopped for the first
time in fifty years, and the house sounded strange without it, the way a house
sounds when a clock is taken down.

Merta had come up on the early train with a speech about letting go. She went
home without giving it, and understood on the way down, somewhere past the river,
what she would say instead at his Vigil, whenever it came, when it was her turn
to Speak: that the stasis unit was never keeping the food. On the far side of
that pantry door it had stayed the wedding night for fifty years — the glasses
poured, the toast still folded, everything still ahead of them. And today, at a
time of his own choosing, with all his guests finally arrived, Josef had let
the night end.
