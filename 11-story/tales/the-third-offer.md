# The Third Offer

*The Wound's edge, fifteen years ago.*

The Watch had lost nine soldiers in a month, and the incursion season showed no
sign of turning, and so the Warden of the Western Watch did the thing that is
forbidden to every soldier on the frontier because it had to be done by someone
and she would not order it.

She walked out at dusk, alone, past the mirror-posts and the salt line and the
last of the watch-fires, to where the grass stops pretending and the ground
begins to hum. She carried no weapon. There is no weapon.

The Grandfather was waiting, because he is always waiting. Thirty feet of elderly
man, seated on nothing, hands folded in his lap like a portrait of patience. His
face is kind. That is the first thing everyone says, afterward, the ones who come
back: *his face is kind.*

"Warden," he said. "You've brought me nothing."

"I've brought you a night of my time," said Roswitha Kravenhold. "I'm told you
value conversation."

"I value trade." He smiled, and the smile was also kind. "But conversation is how
trade begins. Sit."

She did not sit. He did not seem to mind.

They talked — about the season, about the nine dead, about the weather over the
Wound, which is not weather. She asked him nothing, because a question is a want,
and a want is a price tag showing. Near midnight, he made the first offer.

"Your arm," he said. "The one my little cousin took, wearing your sergeant's
face. I could return it. Flesh remembers its shape the way a road remembers its
miles. It would cost you almost nothing. A month of your dreams. You aren't using
them well anyway."

"No," she said.

"Of course," he said, approvingly, as if she'd passed a small exam. Hours later —
the moon low, her prosthetic aching in the Wound's hum the way it always did —
he made the second.

"Your sergeant, then. Aldric. That was his name, before my cousin borrowed it.
He is not so far away as you think. Nothing that walks into the Wound is ever far
away. I could bring him to the edge. You could say the thing you didn't say."

Her breath went out of her slowly, the way you bleed a boiler so it doesn't
scream. "No," she said.

"No," the Grandfather agreed. He looked almost proud.

The sky was greying toward dawn when he leaned down, and this is the part the
Warden has never told anyone, not the Watch, not the capital, not her own house:

"The third bargain is my gift, Warden. No dreams. No years. No price at all."
His kind face came close, and it was still kind, which was the worst thing.
"Ask me what the Wound is *for*, and I will tell you truly."

Roswitha Kravenhold stood in the humming grass with her one hand cold at her
side, and understood, in the way you understand a step that isn't there in the
dark, that she was going to spend the rest of her life not knowing whether this
was the moment she saved herself or ruined everything.

"Nothing is free," she said. "Least of all answers. No."

The Grandfather sat back. He folded his hands. His smile, for the first time all
night, was not kind at all — it was *satisfied*, which is a different thing, and
she has never worked out whom it was satisfied with, or about what.

"Come again in fifteen years," he said. "The offer keeps."

She walked back at dawn. The standing order went up that morning — *no one
speaks to the old man, ever* — and it is the most obeyed rule on the frontier,
because the Warden's face, when she posted it, was the face of a woman who knew
exactly what it cost to obey.

It has been fifteen years.
