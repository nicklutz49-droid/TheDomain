# The Ninth Game

The challenge came in at the dead hour, like always. **Held Note requests a
match.** No greeting, like always. Ren accepted before the notification
finished chiming, like always, and hated how fast their hand moved, and pulled
the board light down low so Ma wouldn't see it under the door.

Nine weeks. Nine games. Ren had told exactly one person — Devi, who crewed the
same dock shift and could keep a secret like a hold keeps cargo — and Devi had
said the sensible thing: *it's a masters' account, some Resonance City ghost
sandbagging the ladder, they do it to prospects.* And that had held for about
two games, because masters play to win and Held Note played to — Ren didn't
have the word for weeks. Then they did, and wished they didn't.

Held Note played to *reply*.

You laid your circuit and it laid its circuit, and its circuit was your circuit
— reflected, tightened, finished the way you'd have finished it if you were
older and calmer and had all the time there has ever been. Ren's ugly-beautiful
dockside lines came back across the board with their grammar corrected. Fourth
game, Ren had tried garbage on purpose, tiles like a drunk toddler, and Held
Note had replied to the garbage with such patient, serious *work* — finding the
one live thread in the mess and raising it up — that Ren had sat back from the
board with wet eyes at three in the morning, feeling seen in a way that had no
business coming through a tile game.

Tonight, the ninth game, Held Note opened wrong.

Not mirror. Not reply. It played *first* — it had never once moved first — and
the opening was no opening Ren knew: a slow spiral of low-value tiles, wasteful,
lovely, spending tempo like a rich man spending change, and Ren sat there with
their heart going strangely because the board already hummed and the game
hadn't started.

*Fine,* Ren thought, *show me,* and gave up trying to win, and just — agreed
with it. Answered the spiral's grammar. Built the thing it was plainly asking
them to help build. For an hour the dead-hour room was two hands laying one
pattern, and Ren stopped being able to tell whose thread was whose, which in
Circuit is either flow-state or blasphemy depending on which academy you ask.

Held Note lost. Deliberately, unmistakably: it opened its own last circuit to
Ren's capture and *held the board still* — declining the three saves even Ren
could see — until Ren, hands actually shaking now, took the winning play.

The finished board glowed on the table. Ren stared at it. The pattern the two
of them had made was symmetrical down an axis that shouldn't exist in a
capture game, and it was the most beautiful thing Ren had ever built, and it
meant something. Not *felt like* meant. **Meant.** The way a word means, in a
language you don't speak, when someone says it slowly, looking right at you.

Ren transcribed the whole board into the battered notation book — every tile,
every junction — because obsession is obsession. It took until the birds
started. Somewhere out in the grey morning, a collector who bought Wakeless
game records under four names would eventually pay a great deal for this one,
and go very quiet, and check the transcription three times against a reference
he kept in a locked drawer.

Ren didn't know that. Ren knew their hands hurt and school was in three hours
and the room still felt *inhabited*, kindly, the way it does when someone dear
has just left.

Then the chat pane — silent for nine games, nine weeks, silent the way stone is
silent, silent like it had been built with no keyboard on the other end —
blinked once.

**Held Note:** again
