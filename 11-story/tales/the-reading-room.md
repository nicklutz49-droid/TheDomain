# The Reading Room

The North Reading Room of the Meridian Archives holds forty desks, and by the
etiquette of archives everywhere, regulars keep their desks the way parishioners
keep pews. Velia Sorn had desk eleven. The quiet woman in reading-gloves had
desk twelve.

Sorn had noticed her the way you notice weather turning: gradually, then all at
once. Small, precise, iron-grey bun, gloves she never removed, requesting boxes
from the 1,940s AC — *adjacent* boxes. For five weeks, whatever Sorn called up
from the Verantum Ascension holdings, the woman at desk twelve had called up
its neighbors. Provenance registers when Sorn had the correction dockets.
Temple circulars when Sorn had the census margins. Two people mapping the same
absence from opposite banks.

Coincidence had died around week three. Sorn had run the woman through every
reader register the Archives kept and found her in all of them and none of
them: always credentialed, always unremarkable, always — when Sorn looked with
her particular eye for the shape of absences — *slightly too well filed.* A
person whose paper trail had no beginning. In the Meridian Archives, that
wasn't anonymity. That was craftsmanship.

On the day it finally happened, it happened over box **VA-1946-Corr-9**, which
the retrieval clerk, sweating slightly, informed them had been requested twice
for the same hour. Desk eleven and desk twelve regarded each other across the
rail.

"Share it," said the woman, mildly. "I read quickly."

They shared it. For two hours the only sounds were paper and the gloved
turning of pages, and Sorn, watching sidelong, learned two things. First: the
woman read *corrections* — not the text, the emendations, tracking hands and
inks the way Sorn tracked absences. Second: at folio 44 — the reconciled
festival accounts, the one where three independent tallies of Audit Square's
crowd had been brought into agreement by a fourth hand — the woman stopped.

She laid one gloved finger on the reconciling entry, very lightly, the way you
touch a bruise to know its edges. And she said, not to Sorn, in a voice like a
page turning:

"There was no fourth clerk."

"I know," said Sorn.

The silence after had a hinge in it. The woman turned — and Sorn would spend a
long time afterward trying to describe her expression to herself, and settle,
unsatisfyingly, on *proofread*: a face checked over once and set down again —
and looked at Velia Sorn with an attention that made the reading room feel
briefly like the bottom of a well.

"How long," the woman asked, "have you known your archive lies?"

"Two years." Sorn's voice held, which she was proud of. "How long have you?"

"Longer." The woman drew off one glove, finger by finger — and shook Sorn's
hand, plainly, like a colleague, and her hand was warm and faintly stained,
after all these boxes, with ink. "We should compare absences, Deputy Archivist.
Yours have shapes. Mine have *dates*. I suspect they rhyme."

They took the corner table by the north window after that, the one desk-pair
where conversation is tolerated, and worked until the closing bell like any two
scholars with one problem. If the woman never gave a name, Sorn — who had
searched every register — had the grace not to ask a question she'd already
failed to answer. She wrote **"the Proofreader"** in her private index and let
it stand.

Above them, along the gallery rail where the High Curator likes to take his
evening circuit of *his* archive, Valdris Crane paused — one hand on the old
brass, the length of the reading room below him: his deputy, and the woman
whose paper trail had no beginning, heads bent together over the Tidy Century.

He stood there a while. A man on a gallery, deciding, in the failing light,
which of the doors he had been saving this was.

Then he finished his circuit, greeted the night clerk by name as always, and
went up to his office, where he unlocked the drawer that held his oldest list —
two names, one province — and added, after forty years, a third line. Not a
name. A question, in his small curator's hand:

*"What does the Kept Word do to a keeper of secrets?"*
