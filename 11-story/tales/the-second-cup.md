# The Second Cup

Oda drew the short straw, which is why she was the one carrying the Harrel
family's debt-token across Freeport Yrshen on the third of the Still Days — a
Bare Day, everyone's faces naked in the streets like a city of strangers, nobody
knowing how to arrange their mouths.

The Debt-Mother's gate was open. The Debt-Mother's gate is always open, which is
its own kind of wall.

She was under the lemon tree, of course, barefaced like everyone and unlike
anyone: Mahalia Wren's bare face turned out to look exactly the way her mask
looked, which Oda would be unpacking for years. The tea was already poured.
Two cups.

The second cup sat across the table, at the empty chair, steam rising.

"You're the Harrels' girl." Not a question. "Set it down, child, the token won't
hatch. Sit."

Oda sat. On the table, held down against the harbor wind by the teapot, lay a
single page of ledger paper, old, soft at the folds as cloth. She did not mean to
look at it. It was upside down to her and she did not mean to look, and she saw
anyway that it was short — a dozen lines in a young hand, and at the bottom, in
ink gone brown, a last entry that was only one letter long.

The Debt-Mother watched her look. Bare Days: nothing to hide behind, in either
direction.

"Everyone wants to know what the Great Ledger says," the old woman remarked,
pouring Oda's tea. "Nobody asks what I owe. Do you know, girl?"

"No, Mother."

"Guess."

"I—" The wind moved the lemon leaves. Across the table the steam of the second
cup bent and straightened. "Something you can't pay?"

The Debt-Mother laughed — a real one, from the belly, and forty years of harbor
politics would have paid gold for the sound. "Something I can't *find*," she
said. "I was nineteen and stupid, and someone did me a kindness on the night
dock. Wouldn't take my thanks. Wouldn't stay for it. I owe a debt to somebody
who won't hold still long enough to be owed — and so, you see" — she gestured
around: the courtyard, the gate, the city, the whole owed world — "I've had to
get so big that one day they can't miss me."

She nudged the second cup a half-inch straighter, the way you straighten a
picture of someone.

"Every Bare Day I pour it. Never yet drunk." She looked at Oda then, bare face
to bare face. "You'll say nothing, and you won't need the reminder, because on
these days everything said is debt-free — and that, child, is the only reason
old women tell the truth on the Still Days. Now. Tell the Harrels their token's
accepted, the interest is forgiven, and the lemon tree needs pruning in Seed-Debt
month; whichever of them comes to do it, I'll count the rest square."

Oda delivered every word. She said nothing else, that year or after. But she is
a dock-clerk of Freeport Yrshen, and she keeps her accounts, and in the private
ledger of things she knows and can never spend, she carries the entry all the
same: *the Debt-Mother sets a cup for Y., and the tea has never once gone
cold before she pours it out.*

She checked, that day. The wind was blowing. The steam rose straight.
