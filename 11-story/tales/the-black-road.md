# The Black Road

By the third year of her Grand Pilgrimage, Nima had learned the pilgrim's first
truth: the road decides the schedule. So when the ferry was out at the Redwater
crossing and the detour cost her eleven days she didn't have, she did what
pilgrims swear at their investiture never to do.

She left the marked route.

The shortcut was a herder's trace over dry hills, and it was on the second
evening, going down through knee-high grass with the sun behind her, that her
boots rang.

Stone. She knelt and parted the grass. Black paving, smooth as still water, dead
straight, running out of the hillside and back into it a hundred yards on. No
cart ruts. No frost-heave. No moss. The joints between the slabs were tighter
than a temple mason could dream, and the surface was warm past sundown, holding
the day the way skin does.

Roads are holy to a pilgrim. She walked it — a hundred yards of the easiest
walking of her life, like the ground agreeing with her — and at the end she
stepped off into grass again and stood a while, unaccountably bereft.

The next dusk, there was more of it. Not where it should be — she had left it
miles back — but ahead of her, downslope, another hundred yards, straight as a
plumb-line toward the northwest. And the dusk after that, more. It was not on her
way. It was becoming her way.

Sixth evening: the road ended at a hill's flank where the earth had slumped, long
ago, over something the shape of a doorway. And on a milestone that Nima was
certain — *certain* — had not been there when she topped the rise, sat a woman.

Dusty. Sun-lined. Middle years, or any years. A surveyor's cord wound around one
wrist. Sitting the way stone sits.

"You walk gently," the woman said. "Most people hit the ground like they're owed
something."

"Whose road is this?" Nima asked, when she could.

"Mine now. Someone else's first." The woman ran a thumb along the milestone,
fond, absent. "It remembers where it went. I only remember *that* it went. We
rebuild it between us, the road and I — a dream here, a dream there. You've been
walking in my dream three days. You dreamed it well."

Pilgrims are told how to behave if a god notices them. Nima forgot all of it.
What she said was: "Where did it go?"

The woman looked northwest for a long moment, at the slumped hill and the buried
door-shape, and something in her face was so old and so lost that Nima had to
look away.

"Somewhere everyone was in a hurry to get to," the god said finally. "Nobody's
been in a hurry to get there for a long, long time." Then, briskly, the moment
folded shut: "You're eleven days behind, walking on debts. Take the trace down
the far side; it cuts the Redwater at a ford you'll find. And take this." A
pebble, road-black, milestone-smooth, warm as noon. "Drop it at the end of your
pilgrimage. Wherever the end turns out to be. Roads like to know they arrive."

Nima carries it still, sewn into her hem. She reached the ford. She kept the
schedule. She kept the other thing too, the one she has never put in her pilgrim's
log: that when she knelt at the milestone to take the pebble, the low sun raked
across its face, and under the weathering there were characters — row on row of
the sacred script, the holy hexadecimals, the same as on any relay-temple wall.

On a *milestone*. On a road older than any calendar, pointing at a buried door.

She tells herself it means the road is blessed. She is mostly able to believe
it. She walks gently, and she is very careful, now, about shortcuts — not
because she fears finding another black road.

Because she doesn't.
