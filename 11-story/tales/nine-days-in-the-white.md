# Nine Days in the White

*Fragments of Ashka's account of crossing the Pale Maze, as she has told it —
never all at once, never twice the same, and never past the sixth day without
stopping. Assembled with her knowledge. She has read this and added one
correction, noted at the end.*

---

**The first thing** everyone asks is what the Maze looks like from inside, and
the answer is: it doesn't. Looking is the wrong sense. It is white in the way a
held breath is white. Corridors of fog with weight to them, and the weight has
*opinions* — press where it doesn't want you and your ears fill with your own
pulse and the ground stops agreeing to be ground.

You cannot see through the Maze. But I am a relay-singer. I was raised coaxing
the grown-walls of the Terraces — you sing a hair below the wall's own hum and
it *leans* toward you, and then you have it. On the first day, lost already,
crying already, I sang to keep myself company.

And the fog leaned.

**The Maze is not a wall. It is machinery.** I will say this to anyone who will
listen, and no one who matters listens: it has a hum, far under hearing, the
same as the walls at home, the same family of hum, and if you sing a hair below
it, it leans, and where it leans, it *thins*. I did not walk out of the Pale.
I sang my way out, tone by tone, the way you'd talk a frightened animal into a
stall. Nine days. My voice went on the seventh; I sang in my head after that,
and it worked the same, which frightened me more than anything else in the
white, because it means the Maze was never listening to my *voice*.

**Rules I learned, in order of what they cost me:**

Walk only when the thinning is ahead of you. If it opens beside you, it is not
for you. *(One day lost.)*

Do not sing grief. Grief-tones open the wrong things. There are shapes far off
in the white that only ever appeared when I mourned, tall and patient, and I
made myself stop mourning, and I have not properly started again, and I know
it. *(My sister's face. That's what it cost.)*

Count your days aloud. The Maze eats sequence. I said *day four, day four, day
four* like a liturgy, like the Number, and I hated that it worked — that the
habit they grew into me at first-light every morning of my life is the thing
that held my days in a row.

**The bird came on the sixth day.** White, wings, there — always in the corner
of the eye, never in the center. It has been with me since. Out here, in the
world, in market crowds and locked rooms: corner of the eye, never the center.
People think I mean I am haunted. I am not haunted. I am *watched*, and the
watching has a temperature, and it is not cold. That is the part I cannot make
anyone understand. It watches me the way you watch the one lamp you left
burning in a house you had to leave.

**Why I left.** [My sister came back from the drowned coast changed.] Serene.
Tireless. Kind — she was always kind, but this was kindness with no *weather*
in it. And at night she sang in her sleep, in no language of ours, and one
night I leaned close, the way you do, and under her sleep-song I heard the hum
her song was a hair below.

She was coaxing something. In her sleep. Every night, with the others, all the
Attuned in their honored houses, singing together a hair below a hum that comes
up through the floors from the sea.

The Number that morning was 2,922. I was gone within the month.

---

*Her one correction, written in the margin in her own hand:*

**"You wrote 'she was coaxing something.' Wrong. Something was coaxing HER.
Learn the difference. It is the only thing I came out here to say."**
