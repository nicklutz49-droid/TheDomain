# Walking Pace

Under the joint-patrol protocol, Shatterbelt pairs are always one from each
power, on the theory that soldiers who share a watch fire don't start wars. So
Sergeant Brama (Empire, 9th Frontier, twenty years in) walked the moonless
stretch past Cairn Forty with Corporal Issa Rahal (Union Architect Corps,
Second Cohort, instruments and opinions), which had been going about as well as
it ever did.

"Your survey stakes are eleven meters into our sector," Rahal was saying.

"The sector moved," said Brama. "The belt breathes. Everything you people chart
out here is a portrait of last Tuesday."

Then the wind stopped wrong, all at once, like a door closing — and both of
them shut their lamps without a word and stepped off the track and stood still,
because they were Shatterbelt soldiers before they were anything else, and the
protocol for a moonless night was written in older blood than either of their
flags.

*Stand still. Lamps out. Count.*

The Column came out of the low ground by the old harvest markers the way it
always comes: at walking pace. Never faster, never slower — the pace of tired
soldiers with a long way to go, which is a pace both of them knew from the
inside. Shapes in files of four. Rahal did not raise her array; instruments
point, and you do not point at the Column. She counted the old way, by fours,
lips moving. Brama counted too. You count so the garrison log stays honest, and
the garrison log stays honest so that no officer in a warm capital can decide
the Column has "dispersed" and reopen the land it walks.

It passed for eleven minutes at eighty meters. The cold coming off it was not
temperature. Brama had done this nine times in twenty years and it did not get
easier; what it got was *quieter*, the way a church gets quieter the older you
get.

Files of four, by fours, and then the last rank, and behind the last rank —

Rahal's breath caught. Brama's hand found her sleeve: *still.*

Behind the last rank walked a single figure in a burned coat, carrying a
waterskin. It did not glide, or flicker, or wear anyone's face. It just walked,
tired, at walking pace, the way a man walks the last miles of a bad road he
has no intention of leaving unfinished. As it drew level with their dark, it
turned its head — and nodded to them. Once. The nod of one soldier to two
others on the same watch.

Then the wind came back, and the low ground was only low ground.

They relit one lamp between them. Rahal's hands were steady, which surprised
her; her eyes weren't, which didn't.

"Fourteen thousand and one," she said finally. "I counted fourteen thousand
and *one*."

"You counted right." Brama unstopped his flask, drank, and passed it over —
Imperial issue, terrible, ceremonial. "The last one's not *in* the count. He's
keeping it." He watched her not understand, and gave her the only briefing on
the subject the 9th Frontier has ever issued, the one that isn't in any manual:

"We count the Column so nobody forgets it's there. He counts it so that
someday it isn't. Different jobs." He took the flask back. "Same watch."

They finished the patrol in the good kind of silence. At the garrison she
logged fourteen thousand even, per protocol; in the margin, where the old
sergeants leave their marks, Brama added the sign the manuals don't gloss —
a small waterskin, tilted, pouring.

It means: *seen to*. It means: *keep walking*. Nobody knows anymore which of
those it started as. Ask the Shatterbelt lifers and they'll tell you it means
both, and stand you a drink, and change the subject.
