# Danel Ost

> ⚠️ **GM-only.** To the world, Danel Ost is the one person who never returned
> from the [Night of Doors](../01-foundation/events/the-night-of-doors.md). The
> truth below is known to no one on Earth — including his sister
> [Corin](corin-ost.md), who keeps his file open.

## Where he is

**Habitat One**, in the [Lunar Complex's](../03-hidden-truth/lunar-complex.md)
civilian contingency zone. Danel Ost — Union census clerk, forty-one at
crossing, meticulous, unmarried, mild — walked through the door that was "his"
twelve years ago and has lived on the moon ever since: the habitability trial's
first and so far only long-term resident.

## His life

The habitat *anticipates*. Rooms warm before he wakes; meals arrive suited to a
mood he hasn't noticed having; the corridor hums its held note. Nothing speaks —
[the Entity communicates with no one](../03-hidden-truth/the-entity.md), and it
has kept that rule even here, which makes Habitat One perhaps the strangest
household in existence: perfect care, total silence.

What it learned quickly: humans need work. Danel was a census clerk, so the
habitat gives him *records* — endless, gentle archives of something (he has
decided not to wonder what) that want sorting, reconciling, filing. He is very
good at it. Some of the records, lately, appear to be *about* him — his sleep,
his appetite, his morale, in a notation he half-recognizes. He files those too.
He has concluded, correctly, that he is the pilot study, and that doing the
filing well is the most useful thing a mild man can contribute to whatever is
being prepared for.

Through his one window: the pale grey plain, and the Earth, blue and entire,
rising and setting on its slow schedule. He has never once been prevented from
standing at the window as long as he likes.

## The letters

He writes to Corin weekly. There is a brass slot by his door; the letters go in;
the slot accepts them. None has ever been delivered. He knows this — he numbers
them, and he knows his sister; she'd have answered letter one — and he writes
anyway, because the slot *accepts* them, and in twelve years it has never once
been full.

Letter 624 begins: *"Dear Corin — the Earth was up again this morning. You would
organize it differently, I know."*
