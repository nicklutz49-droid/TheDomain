# Emperor Hadrik the Chainwright

The emperor who turned conquest into a society — codifier of the **Chain of
Service**, the oath-lattice that still structures the
[Empire](../07-geography/feudal-empire/_index.md).

## The problem he solved

Late Age of Heroes: the Empire was a heap of conquest-era oaths — every lord bound
to whoever had spared, hired, or married them, loyalties crossing like bad rigging.
Hadrik's insight was a smith's: don't untangle the chains, **standardize the
links**. The Chain of Service defined one universal oath-form — service owed
upward, protection owed downward, both *enumerable and heritable* — and re-forged
every existing bond into it over twenty patient years.

## Legacy

- The Chain of Service (canon) — and its cultural shadows: the waste taboo (a link that bears no load shames the chain), lineage friezes, the Feast of Chains.
- The imperial regalia includes **Hadrik's Chain**, a plain iron chain of nineteen links he allegedly forged himself. Coronations end with the new Emperor adding nothing to it — the point being that the chain is finished; the ruler merely wears it.
- His maxim, still carved over the War College gate: *"An empire is not held by the strong. It is held by the well-attached."*

## The god question

Chroniclers can't agree how much of the Chain was
[Thessaly's](../04-divine-beings/the-twelve/thessaly.md) design. Her temple has
never claimed it — which those who know her call the strongest evidence.

## Open threads

- The Chain assumes both directions bind. The colonial "contracted labor" system ([slavery & labor](../10-supplementary/slavery-and-labor.md)) is chain-logic with the protection clause quietly dropped — a legal heresy nobody prosecutes. A reformist heir (Elara?) could make that argument explosive.
