# The First Face

The semi-mythic founder of the Confederacy's mask tradition — and the reason the
masks exist at all.

## The story

Every Confederacy child hears a version: a fisher (or weaver, or debtor — the
tellings vary) survived an encounter with a
[Faceless Beloved](../../06-threats-and-anomalies/hollow/nightmare-born.md) — the
Hollow that wears a loved one's form *with the face missing* — by carving a wooden
face and binding it over the creature's blank absence. Given a face, the story
says, it could no longer take one. It wept through the painted eyes and came apart.

The survivor carved a second mask for themselves that night, so that nothing could
ever take *their* face either. That mask — the First Face — is the ancestor of
every social face worn from Freeport Yrshen to the Thousand Islands.

## What it explains

The mask custom is **apotropaic before it is social**: you wear a face you can
afford to lose. The [Mask-Weavers](../../09-power-and-conflict/secret-societies/the-mask-weavers.md)
claim unbroken descent from the First Face's workshop; mask-stripping as
punishment ([justice](../../09-power-and-conflict/justice.md)) carries its old
terror — the stripped stand *faceless*, and everyone remembers what comes for the
faceless.

## Open threads

- Was the First Face taught? Some tellings add a stranger at the fire who suggests the carving and is gone by morning — folklore's usual seat for [Whisper](../../04-divine-beings/minor-gods/whisper.md) or [Yrshen](../../04-divine-beings/the-twelve/yrshen.md).
- The Mask-Weavers' claim to hold the original First Face is one of the Confederacy's great unverifiable relics. What happens to the entire social order if someone proves it — or disproves it?
