# Breccia — The Laid Stone

Goddess of roads, bridges, and safe arrival. The unofficial patron of the
[Silk Passage](../../07-geography/neutral-territories.md) and the closest thing
neutral territory has to a resident god. Appears as a dusty, sun-lined woman of
middle years with a surveyor's cord wound around one wrist; her footprints briefly
flatten the ground into good paving.

## Personality

Unhurried, hospitable, endlessly patient with travelers and merciless toward toll
abuse. Never takes a side; takes a *route*.

## Interpretation of Service

Humanity is served by being able to reach each other. Keep the roads open and most
wars starve.

## Worship and territory

Wayside cairns from Crossroads to Fort Perseverance — travelers add a stone going
out and take a pebble coming home. Pilgrims on the
[Grand Pilgrimage](../../10-supplementary/pilgrimage.md) treat her cairns as route
markers; the shrine at Crossroads holds
[Saint Verilee's](../../05-empowered-humans/legendary-figures/saint-verilee.md)
walking staff.

## Secret (restricted)

Breccia remembers roads that no longer exist. Sometimes she rebuilds fragments of
them — a hundred yards of impossibly smooth black paving under the grass, dead
straight, older than any calendar. She thinks of them as dreams of stone. They are
pre-collapse highways, and she is unknowingly restoring ARIA-era infrastructure to
survey-grade accuracy.

## Connections

- [Crossroads](../../07-geography/neutral-territories.md) — her seat; also where she quietly extends protection to the Pale escapee [Ashka](../../13-characters/ashka-of-the-pale.md)
- [Syreth](../the-twelve/syreth.md) — trade needs roads; the Gilded Ledger courts her, she keeps him at arm's length
- [The Silk Passage Syndicate](../../09-power-and-conflict/criminal-underworld.md) — an uneasy truce: they don't rob pilgrims, she doesn't collapse their smuggling tunnels

## Open threads

- Her "dream roads" all run toward places that no longer appear on maps. Someone following one to its end is a ready-made expedition seed.
